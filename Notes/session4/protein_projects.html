Projects

Proteins are biopolymers linked to a majority of biological functions. Their biological activity is determined by their 3D shape (also known as “fold”), which dictates their ability of binding to designated biomolecules such as DNA, drugs, or even other proteins. Through these interactions, proteins perform diverse functions, such as transporting substances, recognizing and neutralizing threats, and sensing environmental changes. A key class of proteins, enzymes, catalyse chemical reactions by binding to specific ligands. Enzyme binding often induces conformational changes, which can range from minor side-chain rearrangements to large-scale structural shifts affecting loops or entire domains.

In this project, you will study the structure and dynamics of an enzyme responsible for the catalysis of a reaction on another protein. In the Protein Data Bank, the atomic structure of many enzymes bound to their protein ligand are available (also known as the “bound state”). The question in this project is: how much does the enzyme's structure change between its monomeric ("unbound") state and its ligand-bound state? You will be assigned to a bound-state enzyme structure from the list we provide, and are tasked with performing molecular dynamics simulations to study the enzyme subunit conformational behaviour, in both the bound and unbound states.

During the session 4 workshop you will be assigned to one of the project options below.

Tasks

All required files for this project are located inside the practicals/projects/proteins folder. The following list provides a general description for the tasks of this project, which you should carry out for both of your systems (all proteins and only the chain of interest).

  1. Copy the proteins folder to your Desktop.
  2. For the protein from your option, clean the pdb file. You should have two cleaned files: one with only the chain of interest and one with all proteins
  3. Create a dodecahedron box containing the protein chain.
  4. Solvate the box with SPC water.
  5. Add 0.15 mol L-1 of NaCl and neutralise any excess charge.
  6. Perform an energy minimisation with a force tolerance of 500 kJ mol-1 nm-1.
  7. Equilibrate the system for 100 ps in the NVT ensemble at 300 K.
  8. Equilibrate the system for 100 ps in the NPT ensemble at 300 K and 1 bar.
  9. Run a 50 ns production simulation in the NPT ensemble at 300 K and 1 bar.
  10. Ensure that the protein does not cross periodic boundaries during the period of analysis.
  11. Determine the point where the system reaches equilibrium. Use only the equilibrated portion for subsequent analysis.
  12. Calculate the RMSF of the alpha-carbons.
  13. Perform PCA on the protein backbone.
  14. Inspect the final coordinates and trajectory in VMD and take snapshot(s) that illustrate the important dynamics within the system.
  15. Compare and discuss your simulations with the bound- and unbound-state enzyme structure, providing illustrations from your analysis and VMD as needed.

Input files

Run parameter .mdp files are inside MDP. Use em.mdp for energy minimisation, nvt.mdp for equilibration at constant temperature and volume, npt.mdp for equilibration at constant temperature and pressure and prod.mdp for the production run. Make sure to fill in any missing values for the keywords of the .mdp files that are marked with COMPLETE HERE.

You will find template scripts for submitting your simulation on Eddie inside the project folder.
Remember to run all of your simulations on the Eddie HPC, otherwise your simulation times will be longer than the project work duration!

Cleaning PDB files

To remove all chains except the one of interest, we can use grep and knowledge of PDB file structure. The chain identifier is always in column 5 of the ATOM and HETATM entries, and so we can search the file for where column 5 is the chain letter of interest.

First, identify the number of characters that occur before character we want to search for. Use grep to search for that many characters followed by the chain identifier. E.g. if there were 15 characters and we wanted chain C the command would be:

grep "^.\{15\}C" input.pdb > chain_C.pdb

Then, remove any non-protein atoms as before:

grep -v HETATM chain_C.pdb > protein.pdb

Making an index file

Some of the analysis you are tasked with performing will require you to make an index file. You can do this using gmx make_ndx with the PDB files, and selecting the protein chain you want to analyse.

When using gmx make_ndx you can use the following selection language in the interactive menu:

chain <chain_letter> & a <atom_name>

You may be required to use wildcards (* or ?) as part of the residue or atom name

E.g. to make an index file containing the alpha carbons in chain A, you would select the atoms belonging to chain A, and filter for those with the atom name CA:

$ gmx make_ndx -f protein.pdb -o analysis_groups.ndx chain A & a CA

Then, press q to quit and save the index file

Or to make an index file containing the backbone atoms in chain A, you would select the group that contains the backbone atoms, and filter for those belonging to chain A:

$ gmx make_ndx -f protein.pdb -o analysis_groups.ndx 4 & chain A

Then, press q to quit and save the index file
